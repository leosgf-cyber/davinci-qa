Q01 Color Grading Q&A

My footage looks flat even after applying a LUT in DaVinci Resolve. What's usually going wrong — is it the node order, the color space, or something else?

Expected Answer
The most common culprit is a color space mismatch: if you're feeding log-encoded footage (like S-Log3 or BRAW) directly into a creative LUT that expects Rec.709 input, the LUT gets the wrong signal and the result looks washed out or flat. The fix is to apply a technical LUT (or a CST node — Color Space Transform) before your creative grade to convert the footage to Rec.709 first. A secondary issue is node order: your creative LUT should generally sit after your primary exposure/white balance corrections, not before. Finally, check that your timeline color space in Project Settings matches your monitor's output — if those are misaligned, the image can look off regardless of what you do in the nodes.

Q03 Audio Q&A

I recorded with an external audio recorder and the sync drifts after a few minutes. What causes audio drift, and what's the cleanest fix inside DaVinci Resolve?

Expected Answer
Audio drift happens because your camera and external recorder use independent internal clocks with slightly different sample rates — even a tiny mismatch (like 48000 Hz vs 47952 Hz) compounds over a long take and causes gradual desync. The cleanest fix in DaVinci Resolve is to use Auto Sync Audio in the Media Pool: select both the video clip and the external audio file, right-click → Auto Sync Audio → Based on Waveform. DaVinci analyzes the waveform of the camera's scratch audio and stretches/aligns the external file to match. For ongoing projects, the real prevention is using a timecode sync device (like a Tentacle Sync) or ensuring both devices share the same sample rate settings. If Auto Sync still shows small drift, you can manually use the Speed Change tool with a fractional percentage to correct the stretch.
Q04 Creative / Style Q&A

I want that warm, desaturated film look from A24 movies. What's the general grading approach in DaVinci Resolve, and what makes it look expensive instead of cheap?

Expected Answer
The A24 look leans on lifted blacks, desaturated midtones, and warm highlights — not heavy filtering. Start with your primary correction (proper exposure, neutral white balance), then in the Curves panel, lift the blacks slightly so they never hit pure zero — this mimics film base fog and immediately removes the "digital" feel. Next, bring global saturation down to around 70–80% but push warmth selectively: use the Color Wheels to add a touch of orange/amber in the highlights and a hint of cool green or teal in the shadows (not blue — that's the cheap version). The key to making it look expensive is restraint: the grade should feel invisible. If someone notices your color grade before they notice the story, you've gone too far. Finish with a slight S-curve to add contrast without crushing the shadows, and consider adding a subtle Film Grain node (Effects Library → Resolve FX → Film Grain) to bring texture back after the grade softens things.
Q05 Workflow Q&A

What's the fastest way to organize a multi-camera wedding shoot in DaVinci Resolve before I start editing? I have 4 cameras and 8 hours of footage.

Expected Answer
Start in the Media Pool: create bins per camera (CAM A, CAM B, CAM C, CAM D) and import each camera's footage into its respective bin. Then select all clips across all bins, right-click → Create Multicam Clip Using → Timecode — if your cameras were synced with timecode, this is instant and perfect. If not, use Audio as the sync source (DaVinci's waveform sync works well even across 4 cameras). Once the Multicam Clip is created, drop it on a new timeline and use the Multicam viewer (Workspace → Show Viewer Mode → Multicam) to cut in real time while watching all angles simultaneously — click the angle you want to cut to, and DaVinci writes the edit. Before you start, also do a quick Smart Bin setup: flag any unusable clips (shaky, out of focus, blown exposure) with a Red flag so they're invisible during editing. With 8 hours of footage this upfront organization saves hours of scrubbing later.
Q06 Script Writing Script

Give me a shot list and rough editing script for a 60-second brand video about a boutique fitness studio. Energy should feel motivating but not aggressive.

Expected Answer
SHOT LIST: 1. Exterior — studio facade, morning light, people walking in 2. Detail — hands wrapping tape, lacing shoes, chalk on hands 3. Wide — class in motion (slow shutter or 120fps for fluid movement) 4. Medium — instructor adjusting form, genuine 1-on-1 interaction 5. Close-up — faces: focused, not grimacing — calm intensity 6. Detail — sweat on skin, weights in rack, equipment textures 7. Reaction — smile, fist bump, end of class high energy 8. Lifestyle — member leaving, earbuds in, confident walk --- EDITING SCRIPT (60 sec): [0–5s] Cold open — single slow-mo detail shot, no music yet. Cut hard to music on beat 1. [5–20s] Quick cut montage (1–1.5s per shot) through Shot List 1–4. Build energy with pacing. [20–35s] Slow down — hold on instructor interaction (3–4s). Let the human moment breathe. [35–50s] Pick back up — faces + reactions, cut on movement. Peak energy moment. [50–60s] Resolve to lifestyle shot, fade music slightly, logo/tagline title card. Music note: look for tracks in the 95–110 BPM range with a clear drop around 0:05 — builds without overwhelming.
Q07 Export Q&A

My YouTube exports look soft and over-compressed compared to the timeline. What render settings should I check in DaVinci Resolve, and is there a bitrate sweet spot?

Expected Answer
YouTube re-encodes everything you upload, so your goal is to give it the cleanest possible source file to minimize generational quality loss. Use H.264 or H.265 at a high bitrate — for 1080p, aim for 20–40 Mbps; for 4K, 50–80 Mbps or higher. In DaVinci's Deliver page, set Format to MP4, Codec to H.264, and switch from Automatic to Restrict to with a manual bitrate value rather than using a quality slider — this gives you predictable file sizes. Also check that your Data Levels are set to Video Levels (not Full/Data), otherwise colors will look washed out or clipped after YouTube processes the file. One often-missed setting: make sure your timeline resolution and frame rate exactly match your export settings — any scaling adds softness. If you're still seeing compression artifacts in motion-heavy scenes, bump the bitrate further; YouTube's codec punishes complex motion more than static scenes.
Q08 Creative / Style Q&A

How do I pull off the teal-and-orange look without it feeling cheap or overdone? What's the node approach in DaVinci Resolve that keeps it subtle and intentional?

Expected Answer
The cheap version looks cheap because people apply it as a global hue shift — everything goes orange and teal regardless of context. The intentional approach is to separate skin tones from the environment. In your node graph, use a Qualifier node to isolate skin tones (use the Hue vs Saturation curve to protect the orange-red skin range), then apply your teal push to the shadows and background on a separate parallel node that doesn't affect that selection. In the Hue vs Hue curve, you can push cyans/greens toward true teal and then separately warm the reds/oranges slightly — this is more precise than a global saturation shift. The golden rule: the orange should only live in skin, warm metals, and direct light; the teal should live in shadows and cool ambient areas. If both colors are fighting for the same space in the frame, the grade looks artificial. Keep your saturation conservative (push Hue vs Sat only to around 1.1–1.2×, not 1.5×) and the result reads as color science, not a filter.
Q09 Troubleshooting Q&A

My playback is choppy even on simple timelines with no effects. I've got a decent machine — what's the first thing to check in DaVinci Resolve's performance settings?

Expected Answer
The single most common cause of unexpected choppiness on capable hardware is codec mismatch: if your footage is in a compressed, long-GOP codec like H.264 or H.265 (which is what most cameras shoot by default), DaVinci has to decode many frames simultaneously to reconstruct each frame you see — that's extremely CPU-intensive. The fix is to generate optimized media (right-click clips in Media Pool → Generate Optimized Media) which transcodes everything to an edit-friendly codec like DNxHR or ProRes that can be decoded frame-by-frame. This is the intended editorial workflow. If you don't want to wait for transcode, check Playback → Proxy Mode and set it to Half Resolution or Quarter Resolution as a temporary workaround. Beyond codec, also verify: GPU memory isn't being shared with another application, the Timeline Proxy Mode isn't accidentally enabled at a lower resolution, and that your scratch disk (where DaVinci writes cache) is on an SSD, not a spinning drive — a slow cache disk will bottleneck playback even if your CPU/GPU are fast.
